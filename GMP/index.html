About the group

The Geometric Methods in Physics group, within the Department of Computer Engineering and Mathematics, focuses on differential geometry and its applications to mathematical physics, mechanics, and classical field theory. The guiding idea is simple: the equations describing physical systems (from a particle moving on a curved surface to electromagnetic, fluid or gravitational fields) carry a deep geometric structure. Uncovering it reveals general principles (symmetries, conservation laws, integrability) that are invisible in coordinate-based approaches, and it provides a unified language for problems that, at first sight, look unrelated.

The work is mainly analytical and conceptual, with a clear bridge to physics; some projects involve symbolic or numerical computation. The group collaborates actively with other research groups across Europe within the Geometry, Dynamics and Field Theory Network, and co-organizes a weekly international seminar (Gamma) on geometric methods in physics.

Students joining the group get hands-on experience with modern geometric tools (symplectic, contact, multisymplectic, k-symplectic, Lie groupoids and algebroids, etc.) and apply them to concrete problems. There is plenty of room to shape a project around the student's own interests, whether these lean toward pure mathematics, classical mechanics, field theory, or connections with quantum mechanics and statistics.

Research lines

  1. 01

    Geometric mechanics & field theory

    Geometric formulations of Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics, classical field theories, and their unified Skinner–Rusk framework.

  2. 02

    Contact geometry & dissipative systems

    Contact and k-contact geometry as the natural setting for systems with dissipation: friction in mechanics, non-conservative field theories, and action-dependent Lagrangians (Herglotz's variational principle).

  3. 03

    Symmetries, conservation laws & reduction

    Noether's theorem in general geometric settings, Marsden–Weinstein reduction, and how symmetries simplify and integrate physical models.

  4. 04

    Lie systems

    Systems of ODEs admitting a superposition rule, their geometric description via Lie groups and Lie algebras, and applications to classical mechanics, stochastic and quantum problems.

  5. 05

    Mechanics on Lie groups & algebroids

    Euler–Poincaré and Lagrange–Poincaré equations, reduction by symmetry, and the unifying role of Lie algebroids in geometric mechanics.

  6. 06

    Inverse problem in the calculus of variations

    When can a given system of differential equations be derived from a variational principle? A classical question with surprisingly modern geometric answers.

  7. 07

    Geometric quantization

    Systematic procedures to construct a quantum theory from a classical one (built on symplectic geometry, line bundles, and polarizations) and the bridge they provide between classical and quantum mechanics.

  8. 08

    Information geometry

    Riemannian and Hessian structures on spaces of probability distributions, dual connections, and connections with statistics, machine learning and the geometry of quantum states.
